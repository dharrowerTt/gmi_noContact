Stimulating Investment through the Pilot Auction Facility for Methane and Climate Change Mitigation  



In 2013, the Group of Eight (G8) highly industrialized nations requested innovative approaches to address methane emissions. A report by the Methane Finance Study Group found that a large and growing number of abatement opportunities existed in developing countries; however, in many cases, projects were not implemented due to financial and other barriers. The World Bank created the Pilot Auction Facility for Methane and Climate Change Mitigation (PAF) as an innovative, pay-for-performance model to stimulate investment in projects that reduce greenhouse gas emissions while maximizing the impact of public funds and leveraging private sector financing. The PAF has demonstrated a cost-effective, results-based climate finance mechanism that incentivizes private sector investment and action on climate change in developing countries by guaranteeing a minimum price for carbon credits that represent emission reductions.

While the PAF is a global action, the World Bank believes that PAF’s climate auction model could be applied to support domestic climate targets and actions. Countries have made nationally determined contributions to the Paris Agreement that will require a greater scale and pace of action. Climate auctions are a financing mechanism that can help public funders achieve the best value for incentivizing private sector actions and can help countries achieve their targets in the short-term, while supporting carbon pricing and greater climate ambition in the medium- to long-term.

About the Auctions

The PAF hosts auctions for price guarantees of future emission reductions of methane. Participating companies compete to set a minimum price for carbon credits for methane-reducing projects. The projects that require the lowest guaranteed price for carbon credits win the auction. The PAF’s auctions have been held with multilateral support and have been nearly global in scope. The internet-based auctions allow potential investors from different parts of the world to bid for put options and secure a payment for future carbon credits.

The PAF’s resources are only disbursed after the emission reductions underlying the carbon credits have been independently verified, making climate auctions a pay-for-performance model. Projects have to demonstrate to a third-party auditor that they adhered to a list of PAF’s environmental, health, and safety guidelines, which are largely based on the International Finance Corporation’s Performance Standards. This ensures that the projects and their host sites are well managed.

Measuring Success

The main indicator for measuring progress is the amount of carbon credits redeemed by the auction winners during the different redemption periods. Redemption levels have been high in all redemption windows, demonstrating progress in methane abatement. As of December 2019, winners have redeemed 93 percent of PAFERNs (Pilot Auction Facility Emissions Reductions Notes), representing 10.3 million metric tons of carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e). An independent evaluation published in 2019 found that the PAF demonstrated its relevance for the climate change market and viability of an auction for tradable climate assets, successfully tested different auction formats and generated lessons learned, and proved effective in allocating scarce funding to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and mitigate climate change.

The PAF also measures success through communications with investors and project developers. For example:

  • The majority of respondents indicated that the price guarantee offered by the PAF allowed them to keep their abatement projects operating
  • For the Jeram landfill gas recovery project in Malaysia, PAF’s price guarantee allowed the project to mobilize (or redeploy within the project) an additional 50 percent of funding of the amount received by the PAF
  • The project Central de Resíduos do Recreio Landfill Gas Project (CRRLGP) in Brazil has avoided emissions estimated at 3 million tons of CO2e
  • According to the respondents, the abatement projects have contributed to better air and water quality, improved human health, and expanded economic growth
